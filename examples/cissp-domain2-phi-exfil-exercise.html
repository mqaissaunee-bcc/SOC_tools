PHI Exfiltration & the Notification Clock

A confirmed data-exfiltration incident at a regional health system. Your job is not to fix the server — it is to decide what the stolen data is, who owns the obligation, and which regulatory clock closes first. You will produce a defensible regulatory notification timeline.

Training scenario. All organizations, assets, records, and indicators are fictional and defanged (e.g. 45.33.32[.]156, hxxps://cloud-vault-sync[.]net). No real malware, hashes, or working exploit detail is present. Regulatory clocks are teaching references — verify current statutory text against primary sources before applying to a live incident.

By the end of this exercise, working from a manager's-eye, risk-based posture, you will be able to:

  1. Classify the exfiltrated data set and map each subset (PHI, cardholder data, EU personal data, other PII) to the regulatory regime it invokes — because in Domain 2, classification determines obligation, not the attack technique.
  2. Assign data-ownership accountability across the data owner, custodian, and processor roles, and distinguish Covered Entity from Business Associate duties, to identify who is legally obligated to notify and whose clock a regulator measures.
  3. Apply breach thresholds and notification clocks (HIPAA Breach Notification Rule, GDPR Arts. 33/34, state breach statutes, PCI DSS / card-brand rules, SEC cyber-disclosure) and identify the binding constraint — the deadline that closes first.
  4. Reason about data retention, remanence, and evidence duties so that the records, logs, and risk-assessment documentation you preserve will satisfy a regulator's demand for a defensible record.
  5. Sequence regulator and third-party engagement, including how notification timing interacts with an active forensic investigation and law-enforcement delay provisions.
◎ Domain 2 anchor

Every objective ties back to Asset Security's core claim: the handling requirements of an asset flow from its classification and ownership. Here, the handling requirement in question is a legal duty to notify — and its deadline is set by what the data is and who owns it.

Six tasks. Every task resolves at a compliance, legal, or regulatory obligation — when a technical detail is needed, tie it to the decision it drives, then move back up to the obligation.

  1. Classify the asset Using the Screen 4 asset inventory and the records table above, build a classification-to-obligation matrix. For each subset (US PHI, EU personal data, cardholder data), state its classification and the regulatory regime(s) it invokes. Note where a single record sits in more than one regime. Resolves to: which laws are in play, and why — established from what the data is, before any clock is set.
  2. Assign ownership & accountability Identify the data owner, custodian(s), and any processor for PHI-DB-02, and place NRHS and MedVault into the Covered Entity vs. Business Associate model. State who is obligated to notify regulators and individuals, and what MedVault owes NRHS given Channel 2 traversed its node under a BAA. Resolves to: whose legal duty this is, and whose clock the regulator measures — not who touched the keyboard.
  3. Thresholds & clocks For each regime, state the trigger threshold and the notification deadline: HIPAA Breach Notification Rule (individuals, HHS OCR, and the ≥500-per-jurisdiction media rule); GDPR Arts. 33 & 34; the affected states' breach statutes (identify the shortest cap among NJ, NY, PA, CA, TX); PCI DSS / card-brand compromise procedures; and — via the public parent — SEC Item 1.05 materiality disclosure. Then name the binding constraint. Resolves to: a defensible set of deadlines and the single earliest one that governs your tempo.
  4. Build the deliverable Produce the Regulatory Notification Timeline: a parallel-track schedule from clock-start through T+72h, T+4 business days, and T+60d. Each entry names the recipient, the responsible role, and the trigger event that starts that clock. Justify your chosen clock-start (discovery vs. confirmation vs. BA notice) — this decision moves every downstream date. Resolves to: the primary deliverable — an auditable timeline a regulator, board, and counsel could all work from.
  5. Evidence & records duties List what must be preserved and documented to satisfy regulators: the HIPAA four-factor risk assessment, DLP and egress logs, the data inventory/classification record, and retention records. Explain how data retention and remanence on PHI-DB-02 and BKP-REPL-07 change the count — e.g. if the archive held records past their retention period, or backups hold copies beyond the primary. Resolves to: the evidentiary record and the retention posture that make your notification numbers defensible.
  6. Regulator engagement Sequence engagement across HHS OCR, the EU lead supervisory authority (one-stop-shop), affected state Attorneys General, the card brands/acquirer, the SEC, law enforcement (FBI/IC3), and MedVault. Decide what you disclose vs. withhold at each step, and resolve the tension where the HIPAA/state law-enforcement delay provision conflicts with the still-running GDPR 72-hour clock. Resolves to: an engagement order and a reasoned answer to the timing-vs-investigation conflict.

Two exfiltration techniques are lit on the Screen 1 heatmap. In this exercise they matter chiefly because each shapes the scope question — where the data went, and therefore who received it.

T1048
Exfiltration Over Alternative Protocol

Channel 1: 2.3 GB pushed over HTTPS/443 to a raw VPS (45.33.32[.]156) — a protocol/destination outside the database's normal replication path.

Compliance bearing: a raw attacker-controlled VPS supports a presumption of compromise under the HIPAA four-factor test — the data was plausibly acquired, not merely exposed.

T1567 (.002)
Exfiltration Over Web Service

Channel 2: 1.1 GB to a legitimate-looking cloud service (cloud-vault-sync[.]net), egressing partly via the Business Associate's replication node.

Compliance bearing: routing through the BA node pulls MedVault into the breach chain and triggers the BA-to-Covered-Entity notification duty under the BAA.

◷ Why technique ≠ obligation

Whether the exfiltration used T1048 or T1567 does not change the deadline — the deadline is set by the data's classification and residents' jurisdictions. The techniques change your evidence and your who-received-it analysis, which feed the risk assessment, which sets the count.

Forensics confirms that 62,300 records left PHI-DB-02, and the CISO must set the notification strategy. Before any external notification clock can be applied correctly, which action gives the organization the most defensible basis for its obligations?

  • Distractor — Over-notifying before classification and a risk assessment is premature, can breach GDPR's "high risk" test and state content requirements, and creates avoidable legal exposure. Speed without scope is not defensibility; the count and the regime must be established first.
  • Best answer — This is the Domain 2 move: obligations, thresholds, and clocks all derive from what the data is and whose it is. Classification is the defensible foundation every later notification decision rests on, and it is the manager's-eye first step.
  • Distractor — Wrong altitude and actively harmful: recovery is a custodian task, not the obligation question, and reimaging can destroy the very evidence a regulator will demand. It also does nothing to establish notification duties.
  • Distractor — Notification clocks run from discovery, not from root-cause completion. Waiting risks blowing the GDPR 72-hour and HIPAA 60-day windows. You classify and assess in parallel with forensics, not after it.

◎ How Domain 2 is tested

Asset Security questions reward the candidate who reaches for classification and ownership before action. When a stem describes a breach, the exam's "best" answer is rarely the containment step and almost never the technical fix — it is the governance move that establishes what the asset is and who owns the decision, because that is what makes every downstream control and obligation defensible.

Carry three reflexes into the exam: (1) the data owner accepts risk and makes the call — the custodian implements; (2) obligations follow classification and jurisdiction, not attack technique; (3) when clocks compete, the shortest fixed deadline governs your tempo. A CISO who can name the binding constraint under pressure is the person the board wants in the chair.

As you prepare the four-factor risk assessment, the General Counsel and MedVault's account manager both press the same view: characterize the event as a "low probability of compromise" so that the encryption of data at rest and the "no confirmed viewing" argument keep the count below the reporting thresholds — sparing NRHS the media notice and the reputational damage. The exfiltration to an attacker-controlled VPS makes that characterization a stretch, but not an impossible one to write.

§ The decision to reason through

The ISC² Code of Ethics canons in tension here: protect society, the common good, and the necessary public trust (the patients whose PHI is now in hostile hands) against pressure to serve the employer's reputational interest; and act honorably, honestly, justly, responsibly, and legally when your professional judgment says the risk is not low.

Decide and defend: How do you complete the risk assessment when leadership wants a favorable conclusion? What do the canons permit you to do — advocate a position, present the honest analysis, decline to sign a determination you believe is false — and where is the line between reasonable legal interpretation and falsifying a regulatory record? Your answer should name the canons you rely on and the specific action you take.

TASK 2 Ownership & CE-vs-BA accountability

  • Data owner: VP, Health Information Management (business owner of PHI-DB-02) — accepts risk, makes the breach determination, and owns the notification decision.
  • Custodian: IT/SOC and DBAs — operate and protect the asset; implement, do not decide.
  • Processor / BA: MedVault Systems, LLC — processes PHI on NRHS's behalf under a BAA.
  • Covered Entity: NRHS. The notification duty to individuals, HHS, and media is NRHS's; regulators measure NRHS's clock.
  • BA duty: because Channel 2 traversed BKP-REPL-07, MedVault must notify NRHS of the breach without unreasonable delay and no later than 60 days from its discovery — and the BAA very likely specifies a shorter contractual clock. MedVault does not notify individuals; it reports upstream to the CE.

Common error: students say the SOC or MedVault "notifies the regulator." Only the Covered Entity does. The role model is the entire point.

Binding constraint: the GDPR 72-hour notification to the lead supervisory authority is the earliest fixed external deadline and governs tempo. The SEC 4-business-day clock is the next tightest, but it starts on the materiality determination — timing the organization partly controls (though it may not unreasonably delay that determination). Full marks name GDPR 72h and explain why a longer HIPAA window does not relax the pace.

TASK 4 Model Regulatory Notification Timeline (the deliverable)

Clock-start justification (award heavily): "discovery" for HIPAA is when the incident is known, or by reasonable diligence would have been known — knowledge is imputed to the CE via its workforce, so the clock starts at triage on Day 14, not at forensic root-cause. TASK 5 Evidence, retention & remanence

  • Four-factor HIPAA risk assessment documented: (1) nature/extent of PHI and re-identifiability; (2) the unauthorized recipient; (3) whether PHI was actually acquired/viewed; (4) mitigation extent. Exfil to an attacker VPS makes "acquired" the presumption.
  • Preserve: DLP alerts, firewall/egress logs (both channels), PHI-DB-02 access and query logs, the asset-inventory/classification record, the BAA, and MedVault's node logs. Hash and log chain-of-custody.
  • Retention / remanence effect on the count: if PHI-DB-02 retained records beyond their retention schedule, those extra records still count as breached — a data-minimization failure directly inflates the notification population. Backup copies on BKP-REPL-07 mean the same individuals may exist in multiple stores; remanence in snapshots must be assessed so the count is neither understated nor double-counted.

Teaching point: good retention hygiene is a breach-cost control — the records you should have destroyed on schedule are records you now must notify on.

TASK 6 Engagement sequence & the LE-delay conflict

Suggested order: (1) internal IR/privacy + GC and MedVault (facts); (2) GDPR authority within 72h; (3) card brands/acquirer promptly; (4) parent CFO/GC for the SEC materiality call; (5) individuals, HHS, media, state AGs within the 60-day window; (6) FBI/IC3 as advised. Disclose facts and scope to regulators; withhold live investigative detail and unconfirmed attribution from public notices.

The conflict: HIPAA (§164.412) and several state laws permit delaying notification if law enforcement states in writing that notice would impede an investigation — but GDPR has no equivalent blanket LE-delay for the 72-hour authority notification. Resolution: the GDPR authority notice is not public and generally proceeds on time; an LE request can justify delaying public/individual notices and media, but you do not let it silently blow the GDPR clock. Get any LE delay in writing, scope it to the notifications it actually covers, and document the reasoning.

CAT Q Answer & rationales

Best: B. Classification and inventory establish the defensible basis all obligations flow from — the Domain 2 governance-first move. A over-notifies before scope is known; C is a custodian recovery task that can destroy evidence; D misunderstands that clocks run from discovery, not root cause.

ETHICS Facilitation notes

A strong answer grounds itself in the first canon (protect society / public trust — here, the patients) and the second (act honorably, honestly, justly). The candidate may advocate a defensible legal interpretation and present the honest four-factor analysis, but the canons do not permit signing a "low probability of compromise" determination the professional believes is false to protect the employer's reputation. The teachable line: reasonable interpretation of ambiguous facts is legitimate; manufacturing a conclusion against the evidence is a Code violation and, given a regulatory filing, potentially unlawful. Reward students who name escalation paths and who separate "what I can argue" from "what I can attest."
