Reading the Exfiltration: Foundational Security Concepts on a PCI Breach

A batch account moved 1.8 GB of cardholder data out of the card data environment at 2 a.m. Your job is not to catch the attacker — it is to name the security concepts the incident exercises, then re-read the same evidence as a potential crime.

CompTIA Security+ SY0-701 Domain 1.0 · General Security Concepts Foundational ~35 min Finance / PCI Data exfiltration Lens: Criminal Justice

01 Learning objectives

By the end of this exercise, a student pursuing Security+ (SY0-701) will be able to — every objective tied to Domain 1.0 General Security Concepts:

  • Map a disclosure event to the CIA triad. Given exfiltrated cardholder data, identify which element of confidentiality, integrity, and availability is directly violated and justify the mapping. (1.2 — fundamental concepts)
  • Separate authentication from authorization. Explain how a valid credential can still represent an authorization and accounting (AAA) failure, and identify the record that provides non-repudiation. (1.2 — AAA, non-repudiation)
  • Apply Zero Trust to an egress decision. Describe how policy-driven access control and least privilege at a policy enforcement point would change the outcome. (1.2 — Zero Trust)
  • Classify layered controls by category and type. Sort the controls that touched the incident into preventive, detective, and corrective, and explain the defense-in-depth gap. (1.1 — security controls)
  • Re-read the incident as evidence. Explain how the same foundational concepts (non-repudiation, integrity, logging) support evidentiary standards when an incident may also be a crime. (1.2 — applied through a criminal-justice lens)

03 Scenario

Organization: Garden State Federal Credit Union (GSFCU), a fictional regional financial institution. It stores and processes payment card data, so its cardholder data environment (CDE) is in scope for PCI DSS.

At 07:50 EST you begin your Tier 1 shift. Overnight, a data-loss-prevention (DLP) rule fired on the SIEM. Tier 1 escalated a case to Incident Response tagged Finance/PCI. Nothing about the account looks broken — the credentials were valid and the session succeeded. That is exactly what makes it worth reading carefully.

Timeline (all times EST)

  • 02:11Service account svc_ecom_sync authenticates to CARDDB-PROD-01 from jump host JUMP-DMZ-02. Valid credentials. No interactive human session logged.
  • 02:14Outbound HTTPS session opens from CARDDB-PROD-01 to external host 45.133.216[.]78 on port 443 — a destination outside the CDE and outside any allow-list.
  • 02:29SIEM DLP rule "PAN pattern egress" triggers (Screen 1), severity High. The transfer is alerted but not blocked.
  • 02:41A second channel opens: repeated HTTPS POST requests to the web service hxxps://vault-transfer[.]io/u/9f3a.
  • 03:02Sessions close. Approximately 1.8 GB egressed across the two channels.
  • 07:50You acknowledge the alert; case opened on Screen 3 with sector tag Finance/PCI. Affected asset: the cardholder database.

04 Tasks

Six tasks. Each one ends by asking you to name a foundational concept and defend the mapping — that is the skill Domain 1.0 tests. Tasks 5 and 6 add the criminal-justice lens. Your primary deliverable is the technical analysis report assembled in Task 6.

▶T1 Confirm the alert and map it to the CIA triad

On Screen 1 (SIEM), filter to High-severity alerts and locate the DLP "PAN pattern egress" event. Note its severity, timestamp, and the affected asset. Confirm it matches the case on Screen 3.

Resolve at the framing

State which element of the CIA triad the exfiltration directly violates, and which one or two elements are not primarily affected. Write two sentences: the mapping, and the reason (what actually happened to the data?).

▶T2 Trace the access path against AAA

Open the case on Screen 3 (Incident Response). Review the IOC details and affected assets, and note that svc_ecom_sync used valid credentials with no interactive session.

Resolve at the framing

Break the event into the three legs of AAA. Did authentication succeed or fail? Was the authorization appropriate for a batch account reaching the public internet at 2 a.m.? Which record provides accounting and gives you non-repudiation that this account performed the transfer?

Criminal-justice lens

"Valid credentials" is not the same as "the account owner did it." Note the distinction now — you will need it in Task 5 and in the ethics decision.

▶T3 Apply Zero Trust to the egress

The account authenticated successfully, then behaved in a way no batch job should. Using what you see on Screens 1 and 3, reason about the egress decision.

Resolve at the framing

Explain how two Zero Trust ideas — least privilege and policy-driven access control — would have changed the outcome. Then name one control you would place at the policy enforcement point so that a successful login alone is never enough to reach 45.133.216[.]78.

▶T4 Classify the layered controls (defense-in-depth)

Using Screen 4 (Threat Intel & Endpoints) for endpoint/vulnerability/compliance context and Screen 1 for the MITRE heatmap and detection trail, list the controls that touched this incident: e.g., DLP egress monitoring, endpoint/EDR posture, identity/service-account policy, network egress filtering, SIEM alerting.

Resolve at the framing

Classify each control as preventive, detective, or corrective (Domain 1.1). Then state the defense-in-depth gap in one sentence: which layer detected the transfer, and which preventive layer was missing that let it complete?

▶T5 Re-read the incident as a crime (criminal-justice lens)

Unauthorized removal of cardholder data may be more than an IT incident — it can be a crime and it triggers PCI breach obligations. Shift your reading from "what broke" to "what would this need to be, to hold up outside the SOC?"

Resolve at the framing (dual lens)

Answer three things: (a) What must be preserved so the log evidence remains usable — think chain of custody, timestamp integrity, and who touched what. (b) Which security concepts you already applied (non-repudiation from AAA logging; the integrity leg of CIA protecting the logs themselves) support that evidentiary need, and why. (c) How the SOC's working standard ("high confidence this account exfiltrated data") differs from a courtroom's standard ("beyond a reasonable doubt"), and what that difference means for how you word your report.

▶T6 Produce the technical analysis report deliverable

Synthesize Tasks 1–5 into a one-page technical analysis report a Tier 2 lead could act on. Include, in order:

  1. Summary & timeline — 3–4 lines, defanged indicators only.
  2. Affected assets & data at risk — asset names and the PCI relevance.
  3. Concept mapping — one line each for CIA, AAA, Zero Trust, and defense-in-depth, tied to the evidence.
  4. MITRE techniques — T1048 and T1567, each mapped to a specific line in the timeline.
  5. Evidence-preservation note — the criminal-justice point from Task 5, worded so it distinguishes fact from inference.
Resolve at the framing

The report is graded on whether each foundational concept is correctly named and correctly mapped to the evidence — not on volume of prose. Keep it to one page.

05 MITRE ATT&CK mapping

Both techniques sit under the Exfiltration tactic (TA0010). Map each to a line in the timeline.

T1048 — Exfiltration Over Alternative Protocol

Data leaves over a channel other than the normal command-and-control path. In this scenario: the direct HTTPS session at 02:14 from CARDDB-PROD-01 to the unlisted VPS 45.133.216[.]78:443. Detected by the DLP egress rule on Screen 1; ties to the confidentiality violation.

T1567 — Exfiltration Over Web Service

Data is pushed to a legitimate-looking web/file-transfer service so it blends with normal HTTPS. In this scenario: the repeated POST requests at 02:41 to hxxps://vault-transfer[.]io/u/9f3a. This is why simple IP block-lists are insufficient — a defense-in-depth point for Task 4.

07 Practice question

Part A — Linear multiple choice

Cardholder records were copied from CARDDB-PROD-01 to an external host outside the CDE. Considering only the act of copying the data off the server, which element of the CIA triad is most directly violated?

Part B — Performance-based (matching)

For each observation on the left, select the single foundational concept it best illustrates. Each concept is used exactly once.

1. Roughly 1.8 GB of PAN data was disclosed to an unauthorized external party.
2. The account's credentials were valid, but it was permitted to do far more than its batch job required.
3. No egress policy verified the request before allowing the account to reach the public internet.
4. DLP detected the transfer, but nothing blocked it — only one layer engaged.
5. Signed authentication logs let investigators prove which account performed the transfer.

Think like a security practitioner

08 Exam tip

Domain 1.0 rarely asks you to recite a definition. It hands you a short scenario and asks which concept it illustrates — confidentiality vs. integrity, authentication vs. authorization, preventive vs. detective. This exfiltration is the canonical shape of those items: valid login (authentication succeeded) + wrong behavior (authorization/Zero-Trust failure) + data disclosed (confidentiality) + alerted-but-not-blocked (a detective control without its preventive partner). On the exam, read for the verb — "copied off," "changed," "made unavailable," "logged in as" — and let it point you to the concept. Practice naming the concept before you read the answer choices.

A decision you must reason through

09 Ethics consideration

The logs show svc_ecom_sync — but service accounts are often owned by a named administrator, and that person's name is one query away. It is tempting to write "Administrator J. Reyes exfiltrated cardholder data" because it makes the report feel decisive.

Reason through the decision: valid credentials prove the account acted, not that its owner did — the credential could have been stolen or the account abused. Naming an individual as the culprit before investigation confirms it can cause real reputational and legal harm, and it mirrors the criminal-justice principle you applied in Task 5: presumption of innocence. Weigh that against a competing duty — under PCI DSS, a suspected cardholder-data breach carries notification obligations you cannot quietly sit on. The professional path is to report facts and inferences separately ("the svc_ecom_sync account, owned by J. Reyes, was used to transfer ~1.8 GB; ownership does not establish who operated it"), preserve the evidence, and escalate through the proper channel — not to accuse, and not to bury.

Task 1 — CIA mapping (model)

Confidentiality is the element directly violated: PAN data was disclosed to an unauthorized external party. Integrity is not primary — nothing indicates the records were altered on the server; copying is not modification. Availability is not primary — the data remained accessible internally. Full marks require the confidentiality call and a correct reason for excluding integrity/availability.

Task 2 — AAA (model)

Authentication: succeeded — valid credentials were presented. Authorization: inappropriate — a batch/service account had no business reaching an unlisted internet host at 02:00; the permission set was too broad (least-privilege failure). Accounting/non-repudiation: the authentication and session logs (tying svc_ecom_sync from JUMP-DMZ-02 to the transfer) provide the record. Accept "signed/immutable auth logs" or "SIEM audit trail." Common error: calling this an authentication failure — it is not.

Task 3 — Zero Trust (model)

Least privilege: the account should be scoped to only the internal endpoints its sync job needs — no general egress. Policy-driven access control: each request is evaluated against policy (identity + context + destination), so a valid login alone never grants internet egress. PEP control (any one): an egress firewall/proxy allow-list keyed to the account; a conditional-access / just-in-time policy that denies non-interactive accounts outbound to non-allow-listed hosts; deny-by-default egress from the CDE. Accept any control that blocks based on context, not just successful authentication.

Task 4 — Control classification & DiD gap (model)

Sample classification: DLP egress alert = detective; SIEM alerting/monitoring = detective; egress firewall allow-list / deny-by-default = preventive (was missing/ineffective); least-privilege account scoping = preventive; incident-response containment (isolate host, disable account) = corrective. DiD gap: the detective layer (DLP/SIEM) worked and generated the alert, but the preventive egress-control layer was absent, so detection did not stop completion. Accept reasonable variants; the key insight is detective-fired / preventive-missing.

Task 5 — Criminal-justice lens (model)

(a) Preserve: original logs with intact timestamps, hashed/write-once where possible; document who accessed the evidence and when (chain of custody); avoid altering the affected system before imaging. (b) Concept links: non-repudiation (from AAA accounting) is what lets an investigator attribute the action to the account; the integrity leg of CIA protects the logs themselves so the evidence is trustworthy. (c) Standards: the SOC acts on "high confidence / preponderance" to contain quickly; a court requires "beyond a reasonable doubt." Practical effect: the report should state facts and clearly label inferences, because language that reads as a conclusion of guilt can undermine both fairness and the case.

Task 6 — Technical analysis report (model outline)

A full-credit report includes: (1) 3–4-line summary + timeline with defanged indicators; (2) affected asset CARDDB-PROD-01 and PAN/PCI relevance; (3) concept lines — Confidentiality violated (data disclosed), AAA = auth OK / authz failed / logs give non-repudiation, Zero Trust = deny-by-default egress at PEP, DiD = detective fired / preventive missing; (4) T1048 → 02:14 VPS transfer, T1567 → 02:41 web-service POSTs; (5) evidence-preservation note separating fact from inference. Deduct for missing concept mappings, un-defanged indicators, or accusatory attribution.

Practice question — answer key

Part A (MC): correct answer is B — Confidentiality.

  • A. Availability — incorrect. The data was copied, not deleted or made inaccessible; internal availability is unchanged.
  • B. Confidentiality — correct. The defining harm of exfiltration is unauthorized disclosure of protected data.
  • C. Non-repudiation — incorrect. A valuable supporting concept for attribution, but it is not a leg of the CIA triad and is not what the act of copying violates.
  • D. Integrity — incorrect. Integrity concerns unauthorized modification; copying does not by itself alter the data.

Part B (PBQ): 1 → Confidentiality · 2 → Authentication vs. authorization (AAA) · 3 → Zero Trust / policy-driven access · 4 → Defense-in-depth / control layering · 5 → Non-repudiation.
